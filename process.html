Process

Project Motivation

Our team identified a common challenge faced by students and faculty on campus: a lack of accessible, healthy food options during late hours when dining halls are closed. Traditional vending machines often stock unhealthy options and suffer from poor inventory management, leading to waste and unmet demand. To address this, we envisioned a smart vending machine that would not only provide better snack options but also track item popularity and usage data for continual optimization.

Brainstorming and Planning

We began the project by brainstorming potential solutions to the food accessibility issue and ultimately selected the vending machine concept because of its feasibility, real-world applicability, and opportunity for cross-disciplinary collaboration. Mechanical and Structural Design

The vending machine frame was constructed using black and transparent polycarbonate sheets, reinforced with metal corner brackets for durability. The modular design allows for easy maintenance and part replacement. A hinged access panel on the front and back enables efficient reloading and electrical troubleshooting. Internally, spiral springs driven by servo motors function as the dispensing mechanism, securely holding and rotating to release snacks.

Electrical Integration

We utilized four high-torque servo motors connected to the Arduino board to control each dispensing coil. An IR sensor monitors whether an item has been successfully dispensed. The entire system is powered via an AC-to-DC adapter connected to the wall, regulated by a built-in buck converter on the Arduino to distribute 5V to all components efficiently.

User Interface and Software

A 1x4 membrane keypad was installed on the front panel to allow users to select snacks. The Arduino interprets these inputs and triggers the appropriate motor. Software logic was developed to ensure that only one item is dispensed at a time, with automated retry functionality if an item is not detected. Additionally, the software includes inventory tracking and stock alerts through LED strip color changes.

Data Collection and Analytics

Our system records each transaction with a timestamp and logs the item selected, which allows us to analyze which products are most popular. We use a hashmap to track inventory levels. When stock drops below a set threshold, the LED light strip turns red to signal restocking is needed. This feature improves both operational efficiency and user satisfaction.

Website and Documentation

To document our progress and showcase the final product, we created a GitHub Pages portfolio site. The site includes technical documentation, visuals, team bios, and a demonstration video. This not only helped us reflect on our work but also provided a professional platform to share our project.

Challenges and Adaptation

Throughout the project, we faced technical difficulties, such as managing power limitations and learning new hardware and programming tools. Our team adapted quickly, leaning on our EP mentors and reallocating tasks based on strengths. We worked primarily in the Standard study lounge and had backup spaces planned in case of disruptions. This flexibility helped us stay on schedule and meet all project milestones.

Final Reflection

The vending machine project was a valuable learning experience that simulated real-world engineering challenges. It demanded hands-on application of mechanical, electrical, and software principles, while reinforcing project management, system integration, and teamwork. Most importantly, it showcased our ability to transform classroom concepts into a tangible, functional product that addresses a real campus need.
